\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

We evaluate the \sozkz{} model family against \numModels{} models across \numTasks{} tasks spanning perplexity, classification, reading comprehension, and structured prediction.
Our findings reveal a clear pattern: the dedicated Kazakh tokenizer grants \sozkz{} a substantial advantage on bits-per-byte, and the 600M model is competitive on classification tasks despite being an order of magnitude smaller than leading multilingual alternatives.
However, knowledge-intensive tasks such as multiple-choice question answering and reading comprehension favor larger models, confirming that world knowledge scales with parameter count.

\cref{tab:comparison} presents the full results and \cref{fig:comparison} visualizes the accuracy tasks.

\paragraph{Bits-per-byte.}
\sozkz{}-600M achieves a BPB of \bpbSozKZSixHundredM{}, the lowest among all models including GPT-OSS-120B (\bpbGptOssOneTwentyB{}).
Even the smallest \sozkz{}-50M (\bpbSozKZFiftyM{}) outperforms Llama-3-1B (\bpbLlamaThreeOneB{}) and Qwen-0.5B (\bpbQwenZeroFiveB{}) on this metric.
This advantage stems directly from the dedicated Kazakh tokenizer, which encodes Kazakh text far more efficiently than multilingual alternatives (see \cref{sec:tokenizer-analysis}).

\paragraph{Sentiment analysis.}
\sozkz{}-600M achieves \accSentimentSozKZSixHundredM{} on sentiment classification, surpassing Llama-3-8B (\accSentimentLlamaThreeEightB{}) and Mistral-7B (\accSentimentMistralSevenB{}).
This result is notable: a 600M-parameter model trained exclusively on Kazakh outperforms models with $13\times$ more parameters on a task that relies primarily on surface-level linguistic patterns rather than factual knowledge.
The 300M model (\accSentimentSozKZThreeHundredM{}) also exceeds several multilingual baselines including Gemma-2B (\accSentimentGemmaTwoB{}) and Qwen-1.5B (\accSentimentQwenOneFiveB{}).

\paragraph{Named entity recognition.}
On NER, \sozkz{}-600M scores \accNerSozKZSixHundredM{}, which is competitive with Llama-3-3B (\accNerLlamaThreeThreeB{}) and Mistral-7B (\accNerMistralSevenB{}).
The 300M model (\accNerSozKZThreeHundredM{}) exceeds both Llama-3-1B (\accNerLlamaThreeOneB{}) and Qwen-0.5B (\accNerQwenZeroFiveB{}).
NER performance benefits from the tokenizer's ability to preserve Kazakh morphological boundaries, reducing subword fragmentation of entity spans.

\paragraph{Topic classification (SIB-200).}
\sozkz{}-600M achieves \accSibTwoHundredSozKZSixHundredM{} on SIB-200 topic classification.
While this matches Llama-3-1B (\accSibTwoHundredLlamaThreeOneB{}), it falls below Qwen-1.5B (\accSibTwoHundredQwenOneFiveB{}) and larger models.
Topic classification requires broader world knowledge to distinguish between domains, where larger multilingual models have an advantage from exposure to diverse training corpora.

\paragraph{Multiple-choice QA.}
Knowledge-intensive tasks expose the expected limitation of small models.
\sozkz{}-600M scores \accMcQaSozKZSixHundredM{} on multiple-choice QA, well below the random baseline of 25\% for four-choice questions.
This is consistent across all small models: Qwen-0.5B scores \accMcQaQwenZeroFiveB{} and Llama-3-1B scores \accMcQaLlamaThreeOneB{}.
Strong MC QA performance requires both factual knowledge and reasoning, capabilities that scale with model size---Gemma-9B reaches \accMcQaGemmaNineB{} and GPT-OSS-120B achieves \accMcQaGptOssOneTwentyB{}.

\paragraph{Reading comprehension (Belebele).}
A similar pattern holds for Belebele reading comprehension.
\sozkz{}-600M achieves \accBelebeleSozKZSixHundredM{}, above the 150M (\accBelebeleSozKZOneFiftyM{}) and 50M (\accBelebeleSozKZFiftyM{}) models but below Qwen-0.5B (\accBelebeleQwenZeroFiveB{}) and Llama-3-1B (\accBelebeleLlamaThreeOneB{}).
Reading comprehension requires cross-sentence reasoning over passages, where larger context windows and richer representations provide a clear advantage.

\paragraph{Efficiency framing.}
Comparing \sozkz{}-600M directly against Llama-3-8B, the largest open-weight competitor, we observe that \sozkz{}-600M delivers \accSentimentSozKZSixHundredM{} vs.\ \accSentimentLlamaThreeEightB{} on sentiment (slightly higher) and \accNerSozKZSixHundredM{} vs.\ \accNerLlamaThreeEightB{} on NER (comparable), with $13\times$ fewer parameters.
On BPB, the gap is dramatic: \bpbSozKZSixHundredM{} vs.\ \bpbLlamaThreeEightB{}.
The trade-off is clear: knowledge-intensive tasks require scale, but surface-level NLP tasks can be addressed efficiently by smaller, language-specialized models.

\end{table}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{fertility_bar}
\caption{Tokenizer fertility comparison on Kazakh text.
  The \sozkz{} tokenizer (blue) achieves substantially higher characters-per-token than all multilingual alternatives, indicating more efficient encoding of Kazakh morphology.}
\label{fig:fertility}
\end{figure}

The \sozkz{} tokenizer encodes Kazakh text at a fertility of 5.82 characters per token (\cref{tab:fertility}, \cref{fig:fertility}), more than $2\times$ the rate of the best multilingual tokenizer (Gemma at 2.53 characters per token).
This difference has a direct impact on model efficiency: the same Kazakh sentence requires roughly half as many tokens when processed by the \sozkz{} tokenizer compared to multilingual alternatives.

The fertility advantage arises from vocabulary allocation.
Multilingual tokenizers such as Llama~3 (128K vocabulary) and Qwen~2.5 (152K vocabulary) distribute their token inventory across 100+ languages, leaving relatively few entries dedicated to Kazakh subwords.
The \sozkz{} tokenizer, with its 50K vocabulary trained exclusively on Kazakh text, allocates every token to Kazakh morphemes and common word forms.
This is particularly beneficial for Kazakh, an agglutinative Turkic language where words are formed by successive suffixation---a monolingual tokenizer can learn common suffix chains as single tokens rather than fragmenting them.

The fertility advantage propagates directly to BPB: fewer tokens to predict the same text means lower per-byte surprisal.
This explains why \sozkz{}-600M achieves the best BPB (\bpbSozKZSixHundredM{}) despite being far smaller than competitors like Qwen-7B (\bpbQwenSevenB{}) and Gemma-9B (\bpbGemmaNineB{}).
It also translates to practical inference benefits: fewer tokens per input means proportionally faster processing and lower memory usage (\cref{sec:efficiency}).

\subsection{Scaling Analysis}
\label{sec:scaling}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{scaling_own}
\caption{Scaling behavior of \sozkz{} models (50M--600M parameters).
  BPB decreases consistently with model size, following an approximate power-law relationship.
  Accuracy metrics show diminishing returns beyond 300M parameters on several tasks.}
\label{fig:scaling-own}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{scaling_all}
\caption{Performance vs.\ model size across all evaluated models.
  \sozkz{} models (blue) are plotted alongside multilingual competitors.
  GPT-OSS-120B is excluded for readability.}
\label{fig:scaling-all}
\end{figure}

\cref{fig:scaling-own} shows the scaling behavior across our four model sizes.
BPB improves consistently from \bpbSozKZFiftyM{} (50M) to \bpbSozKZSixHundredM{} (600M), following an approximate power-law relationship consistent with neural scaling laws~\citep{kaplan2020scaling}.
The improvement from 50M to 150M (a $3\times$ increase in parameters) reduces BPB by 15\%, while the improvement from 300M to 600M (a $2\times$ increase) yields a further 13\% reduction, suggesting that diminishing returns have not yet set in for BPB at this scale.

On accuracy tasks, the picture is more nuanced.
Sentiment analysis shows steady gains: from \accSentimentSozKZFiftyM{} (50M) to \accSentimentSozKZSixHundredM{} (600M), an absolute improvement of over 24 percentage points.
NER follows a similar trend, rising from \accNerSozKZFiftyM{} to \accNerSozKZSixHundredM{}.
However, MC QA scores remain below the random baseline even at 600M (\accMcQaSozKZSixHundredM{}), indicating that factual knowledge acquisition requires either more parameters or more diverse training data.

\cref{fig:scaling-all} places \sozkz{} models in the context of multilingual competitors.
The \sozkz{} family occupies a distinct region: lower BPB than comparably-sized multilingual models (tokenizer effect) but lower accuracy on knowledge-intensive tasks.
This confirms that the tokenizer advantage and the knowledge disadvantage are orthogonal effects---the former is a property of the vocabulary, while the latter is a property of the training data diversity and model capacity.

From a Chinchilla-optimal perspective~\citep{hoffmann2022chinchilla}, our 600M model was trained on approximately 9B tokens, yielding a tokens-to-parameters ratio of 15.3:1.
The Chinchilla-optimal ratio is approximately 20:1, suggesting that the model is slightly undertrained.
A fully data-optimal training run would require approximately 12B tokens, which is achievable given the 13.7M-document corpus used for training.

\label{sec:efficiency}

The efficiency analysis in \cref{tab:efficiency} quantifies the practical deployment advantage of small, specialized models.
\sozkz{}-600M achieves an inference latency of 178.6\,ms for 128 tokens, compared to 912.7\,ms for Llama-3-8B---a $5.1\times$ speedup.
Throughput follows accordingly: 717\,tok/s for \sozkz{}-600M versus 140\,tok/s for Llama-3-8B, a $5.1\times$ improvement.

The memory footprint difference is equally significant.
\sozkz{}-600M requires 1,523\,MB of GPU memory, while Llama-3-8B requires 16,234\,MB---a $10.7\times$ reduction.
This means \sozkz{}-600M can run on consumer-grade GPUs with 4\,GB of VRAM, whereas Llama-3-8B requires at least a 24\,GB GPU or quantization.

Combined with the competitive accuracy on sentiment (\accSentimentSozKZSixHundredM{} vs.\ \accSentimentLlamaThreeEightB{}) and NER (\accNerSozKZSixHundredM{} vs.\ \accNerLlamaThreeEightB{}), these efficiency gains make a compelling case for deploying specialized small models in production Kazakh NLP systems where latency, throughput, and hardware cost are primary constraints.
The tokenizer advantage further amplifies the efficiency: the same Kazakh text requires roughly half as many tokens to process, effectively doubling the throughput advantage in real-world applications.
